Grants.Gov

Government Website Redesign

Overview


Grants.Gov is difficult to navigate due to the overflow of information, making the experience on this website overwhelming and difficult to complete tasks.

Role


Eman Khallaf: UX/UI Designer - Wireframing, Prototyping
Tracey Tran: UX/UI Designer - User Research, Usability Tests

Design Solution


Redesign the Grants.Gov desktop and mobile website to be responsive, cohesive, and coherent.

Tools

Timeline


4 Weeks

Results and Insight

Site Audience

Users had various opinions on Grants.Gov's audience, ranging from librarians, museum curators, students, and grant writers.

Tasks

Some users had a difficult time completing their tasks. Some were unable to complete tasks.

Appearance

The site was text heavy, had too many navigation points, and had confusing language.

Redline Analysis

  • Streamlining the navigation to one central location.
  • Unifying the process to accessing grants information.
  • Simplifying user understanding of grant application process.

Definition

User Persona

  • User Insight


    Tina, a development coordinator at a food scarcity nonprofit organization, is seeking funding opportunities on Grants.Gov so that her organization has more money to feed the underserved population in the community.

  • Problem Statement


    As a nonprofit development coordinator, Tina has to juggle numerous responsibilities at her job. Although grant making is a large portion of the job, she has little time to scope out prospective grants because of her other tasks.

  • Value Proposition


    Tina, a development coordinator at a food scarcity nonprofit organization, struggles to find the right funding opportunities on Grants.Gov due to challenges in finding information and difficulties in completing tasks on the current website. We will address this in the redesign by simplifying the understanding of the grants process and who is eligible for grant making opportunities.

Style Guide

Purple ended up being our main color due to its association with creativity and wisdom. Applicants using Grants.Gov's services are often well-regarded thought leaders in their respective industries and sectors, coming from prestigious institutions and universities. Additionally, we wanted to bring out the creative aspect of grant-writing, as applicants use their words to develop compelling proposals. Coupled with vibrant pinks, greens, purples, and yellows, we wanted to give Grants.Gov a start-up aesthetic. As a grant-writer myself, organizations of varying sizes embody the entrepreneurial spirit through mission-driven organizations.

Brand Identity and Guide

Grants.Gov primarily consists of various shades of purple and plenty of white space. The dark purple is the most dominant, used for body text and navigation. The blueberry and grayish purples serve as the color for sub-headers for the secondary pages and smaller Calls to Action.

Unlike most government websites, there are few stock photos that can depict grantmaking in an engaging way outside of a person typing on the computer or writing on paper. Colorful illustrations (inspired by websites for startups and project management services) were the solution to this problem. The sprinkles of pink, orange, lime green, and sunflower yellow became the main palette for these illustrations and created a friendly and welcoming atmosphere for site visitors.

Typography and Iconography

Photography/Images

Grants.Gov's font is Avenir, a san serif typeface. Avenir is the French word for "future," which aligns with Grants.Gov's position in the sphere of government websites as a hub for experimentation, innovative thinking, infrastructure building, and systemic change.

Avenir's simplicity allows users to focus solely on the important information and avoid distractions by using only one font with various weights, which is crucial for grantmakers who need to closely follow application instructions for a successful grant proposal submission.

Iconography plays a factor in the way Grants.Gov users interact with the website. The majority of the icons are shades of purple that blend into with the rest of the website aesthetic. The exceptions to this design choice are the Search and Check icons. In addition to purple, Search can also be pink to emphasize the Call to Action for grantmakers to seek funding opportunities. The Check icon is green to indicate "positive" feedback (i.e. submit, completion, etc.).

    Reflection and Next Steps

    Reflection

    Overall, we had a lot of fun redesigning Grants.Gov. In addition to the UI skills we improved on in this unit, our skill sets complemented each other very well. One of us comes from a graphic design background while the other has experience with grants research and writing, providing us a massive advantage for this case study.

    While we were concerned that class moved remotely about a week into this case study, we quickly embraced the transition thanks to the convenience of video conferencing apps like Google Hangouts and Zoom as well as digital collaboration tools such as Miro, Invision, and Adobe XD. These tools really pushed us to be in constant communication with each other.
